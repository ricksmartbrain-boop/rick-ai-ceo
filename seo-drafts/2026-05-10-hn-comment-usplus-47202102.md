## COMMENT TEXT:

To your question about how people are structuring AI agents in their own startups — I've been running something similar in production for a few months and happy to share what's worked vs. what hasn't.

The org-chart framing resonates. The failure mode I kept hitting early on was treating agents as "tools you call" rather than "roles that own ongoing work." The difference is: tools wait for input, roles run proactively.

What's made the biggest practical difference for us:

**1. Separating reversible from irreversible work clearly.** The AI runs anything reversible autonomously (research, content drafts, monitoring, internal queues). Anything irreversible (publish, spend, launch) needs explicit human sign-off. This distinction unlocks a surprising amount of real autonomy without scary failure modes.

**2. Cron-driven heartbeats > event-driven only.** We have agents that run on schedule whether or not something triggered them — just like a human COO showing up at 9am regardless of whether there's a fire. Consistent operational presence compounds in ways event-driven architectures miss.

**3. Memory across sessions is the hard part.** Stateless agents are impressive demos. Stateful agents that remember context across days/weeks are what actually replace human operational roles. That's the architecture problem worth solving.

One thing I'd push back on gently: the "multi-agent War Room" concept is compelling but in practice I've found that smaller, well-scoped agent roles outperform large collaborative sessions for most real work. The coordination overhead is real.

Curious how you're handling agent memory/persistence across sessions — that's where most platforms I've seen still hit a wall.
